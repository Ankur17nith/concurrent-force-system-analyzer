📘 Mechanical Theory — Concurrent Force Systems

1. What is a Concurrent Force System?

A concurrent force system is a set of two or more forces whose lines of action all pass through a single common point (the point of concurrence). Since all forces act at the same point, they produce no net moment (torque) about that point — only translation is possible.

Examples in engineering: forces at a pin joint, cable tensions at a pulley, leg forces on a bridge truss node.

2. Force Resolution into Rectangular Components

Any planar force F acting at angle θ from the positive x-axis can be replaced by two equivalent forces along the coordinate axes:

Fx = F · cos(θ)  ← Horizontal (x) component
Fy = F · sin(θ)  ← Vertical (y) component

Why cosine for x? In the right-triangle formed by the force vector, the horizontal side is adjacent to angle θ. cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse → adjacent = F · cos(θ).

Why sine for y? The vertical side is opposite to angle θ. sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse → opposite = F · sin(θ).

3. Resultant Force

The resultant is the single equivalent force that has the same effect as all the individual forces combined.

ΣFx = Fx₁ + Fx₂ + Fx₃ + … (sum all x-components)
ΣFy = Fy₁ + Fy₂ + Fy₃ + … (sum all y-components)
R = √(ΣFx² + ΣFy²) (Pythagorean theorem)
θ_R = tan⁻¹(ΣFy / ΣFx) (direction angle)

4. Static Equilibrium Conditions

A concurrent force system is in static equilibrium when the vector sum of all forces is zero. For a 2-D system, this gives two scalar equations:

ΣFx = 0 (net horizontal force = zero)
ΣFy = 0 (net vertical force = zero)

These are also known as Lami's conditions for concurrent force equilibrium and are a direct application of Newton's First Law of Motion.

5. Equilibrant Force

If the system is NOT in equilibrium, an additional force called the equilibrant must be applied to bring the system to rest. The equilibrant is equal in magnitude to the resultant but acts in the exact opposite direction:

|F_eq| = |R|
θ_eq = θ_R + 180°

6. Sign Convention Used in This Tool

  • Angles measured counter-clockwise from positive x-axis (East).
  • Forces with positive Fx point right (East); negative Fx point left (West).
  • Forces with positive Fy point up (North); negative Fy point down (South).
  • Angles resulting from atan2 are in the range −180° to +180°.
